Key Features

I started Sunken Seas as a full project, but as I was developing the prototype, I found that learning about the shader system was much more important skill to develop to further my career than the project itself. I did create a few basic mechanics for Sunken Seas, such as player swapping and side scroller movement. However, I decided the shaders had more impact. A lot of the requirements for the shader that I envisioned were relatively difficult to achieve. I had to start from the ground and work my way up, learning both the unity shady graph as well as the programmable shaders so that I could merge the two together to achieve the desired effects I wanted.


My major roles and tasks done on this project include:

- Utilized Unity’s Shader Graph to build creative shaders
- Merged both Shader Graph and programmed shader effects to create desired shaders
- Developed a stencil buffer system for shaders
- Implemented visual components of color and transparency
- Created vertex displacement shaders effects

Unity Shader Graph


The Unity Shader Graph is a key component for artistic game development within Unity. Before the Unity Shader Graph, developers used pure code to generate shaders. This has been transformed into an easier to use “drag and drop” graph system. While there are certainly gaps in this system’s abilities, it does provide a very useful role in development that is the perfect mix for both programmers and artists, allowing them to achieve artistic shaders with ease. I found that learning the system can be a bit challenging due to the vast number of options that are presented to you. As I grew more comfortable with the system, I discovered very intriguing and interesting options available to create different special effects or designs on shaders. The main shader that I created for Sunken Sea’s ocean scene used a mixture of techniques from the Unity Shader Graph with vertex displacement that flows with time, transparency with coloration and many other tools provided to me. Overall, I think I was able to make something wonderful that I never would’ve been able to achieve with programming alone.



Stencil Buffers & Layering


During development, I foresaw the Sunken Seas shader as a moving ocean that ebbed and flowed up against the boat in a 3D format. The major issue that presented itself here was the interior of the boat would be filled with the ocean’s water in a regular environment as the boat was submerged. Water flowing inside the boat instead of the boat being inside of the water took a little bit of research to understand how to solve, leading me to learn about stencil buffers. Stencil buffers are a technique used to reorganize the drawing order. I created a stencil buffer outline of where the ship is and told the game not to render the shader in that area, allowing me to place the ship inside of the water while not having the water flow through the ship. My next issue to solve was layering the shader on top of another shader as to provide a textured surface of the water while having a clear blue hue to the side view. This took a little bit of working with mathematics to understand how to duplicate the vertex displacement on a new scale, but I managed to layer one shader on top and another that uses the same time displacement with modified movement to sync up to each other and appear as one ocean.
