Key Features

The Mountain was a university project from which I decided to center around game design. At the time, I was the only member in my group experienced with game design so I knew that it would be a challenging project. However, I saw this as a chance to work on a passion project which would implement designs from survival games I have enjoyed in the past to create something fun to play that was my own. Since this project had very few restrictions, I realized how easy it can be to fall into a trap of scope creep I found myself learning many tools on how to identify an overly large idea and bring it back down to its core values. While a playable demo was released, we decided a full version was not worth pursuing.


My major roles and tasks done on this project include:

- Created a large range of mechanics within the Unity engine
- Randomized 3D dungeons with dynamic room generation
- Developed skills on atmospheric and lighting design
- Learned how to create enemy AI with state machines
- Practiced developing basic animations and visual effects
- Designed and produced common staples of the first-person genre such as, movement, abilities and ray cast detection

Procedurally Generating Dungeons & Rooms


A core feature that I wanted to have in The Mountain was procedurally generated dungeons and rooms. I find procedural generation very interesting as it allows for even the developer to become surprised and enamoured by their own game, especially while playtesting. I love the idea of no experience being the same. However, handcrafted experiences do have their place. The systems I used were designed to mix and match both ideologies of level design together when designing the dungeon and room. The general idea behind the dungeon system was to take pre-fabrication rooms and hook them up with hallways to one another, expanding them outwards from a starting node located in each doorway of a room. From there, an attempt to spawn a room will occur and the spawn collides will see if the next room is available to be placed without an intersecting of each other. Once all the rooms are placed the rooms together, the randomization system kicks in to create different pre-built types of the same room which include different loot placements, furniture, and enemy spawns. This allowed for a mixture between procedural generation and handcrafted design, giving the player a sense of both continuity and change during their dungeon crawling experience.



First Person Systems


There were many first-person systems that needed development to make The Mountain. Creating the movement system was basic but I also needed to add movement abilities so that combat would be a little bit more interesting with a skill dodge. From there, I had a lot of interactables in the game, including hit boxes for the players, enemies, weapons and pick-up collider is on items. After that, I need to develop raycast systems from the player to interact with stationary interactables such as the workbench or the firepit. Lastly, I had to develop the UI system that interconnects with everything in the game. There were many other mechanics added to the game, but I found that these first-person systems have helped me develop my skills the most.



Developing Knowledge in Other Trades


A huge part about developing The Mountain was working with other developers who had little experience in game development, design, art or sound roles. This led me to deepen my knowledge on other forms of game development beyond programming. I developed skills in art manipulation with Photoshop, audio edits with Audacity, UI and UX creation, level building, lighting design and even story writing. There was a lot to learn and work to deepen my skills on these new roles that I filled. I gained a deeper appreciation for how each of these roles interact with my main roles as a programmer. The more I developed in other roles the better I was able to streamline my work into different sectors of game development.
